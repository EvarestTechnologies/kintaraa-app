# The Problem

Gender-based violence is widespread, underreported, and chronically under-resourced in its response systems. The gap between a survivor seeking help and receiving it is not primarily a resource gap — it is a coordination gap.

## Fragmented response systems

When a survivor reaches out for help, they typically encounter a system that was not designed for them:

- A hotline connects them to one organization, which may refer them to another, which may refer them further
- Each referral requires the survivor to re-tell their story
- No single actor has visibility into what help has been given or what is still needed
- Providers work in silos — the hospital does not know what the legal aid clinic is doing; the counselor does not know when the survivor has been assigned a police officer

The result: survivors fall through the cracks. Cases are duplicated, dropped, or delayed. Providers waste time on coordination they should not have to do manually.

## The reporting barrier

Many survivors do not report at all. The barriers are well-documented:

- Fear of not being believed
- Fear of retaliation from the perpetrator
- Shame and stigma
- Not knowing what services exist or how to access them
- Fear that reporting will expose their identity or location
- Practical barriers: no phone, no transport, no time away from the perpetrator

Existing digital tools typically require account creation, real-name identification, and an internet connection — each of which is a barrier for the most vulnerable survivors.

## Provider capacity is invisible

From the provider side, there is no shared view of who is available, who is overloaded, or which cases need attention most urgently. Assignment of cases is done manually, by phone, or not at all. A community health worker in the field has no way to know whether the survivor they just visited has already been contacted by a counselor or is waiting for a legal referral.

## What this costs

- Delayed access to medical care after sexual violence (the window for post-exposure prophylaxis is 72 hours)
- Legal cases weakened by late evidence collection
- Survivors who disengage from the system before receiving help
- Provider burnout from coordination overhead
- No data on outcomes, so no ability to improve the system

## The specific gap Kintaraa addresses

Kintaraa does not try to solve the root causes of gender-based violence. It addresses the operational failure in the response system: the absence of a shared, real-time coordination layer that connects survivors to providers and providers to each other.

The platform is designed for contexts where:
- Survivors primarily use mobile phones
- Providers are distributed across multiple organizations
- Internet connectivity is intermittent
- Anonymity is a safety requirement, not a preference
- Case data must be kept private and access-controlled
